Theory Behind the Simulation

This simulation models the behavior of a sealed 1-L autoclave containing water and air. Key principles include:

  • Water expansion - Water expands as temperature increases, reducing the gas volume
  • Ideal gas law - PV = nRT, gas pressure increases as volume decreases or temperature increases
  • Henry's law - Gas solubility decreases with increasing temperature
  • Antoine equation - Describes the relation between vapor pressure and temperature
  • Raoult's law - The partial pressure of each gas contributes to the total pressure

As temperature rises, water vapor pressure increases significantly, contributing to the total pressure in the container. At the same time, gases become less soluble in water, changing the distribution between dissolved and gas phases.

How to Use This Simulation

  1. Adjust the initial water volume slider to set the starting water level at 25°C
  2. Use the temperature slider to heat the container from 25°C to 250°C
  3. Observe how the water expands and reduces the gas volume
  4. Note the pressure increase shown on the gauge and in the display
  5. Watch how gas dissolution changes with temperature in the bar chart
  6. Try different initial water volumes to see how it affects the system

At high temperatures, the pressure can become very high, demonstrating why safety measures are important in pressurized systems like autoclaves and pressure cookers.

About This Simulation

This interactive simulation demonstrates the thermodynamic principles governing the behavior of water and air in a sealed container as temperature changes.

The simulation is based on a Wolfram CDF demonstration originally created by Derek M. Machalek and Rachael L. Baumann.

Applications of this principle include:

  • Autoclaves and sterilization equipment
  • Pressure cookers
  • Industrial reactors and closed systems
  • Understanding gas laws and thermodynamic principles

The simulation simplifies the complex mathematical relationships to provide an intuitive understanding of the system behavior.
