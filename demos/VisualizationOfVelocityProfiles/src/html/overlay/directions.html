Directions

Learning objective:
(1) Understand force balances during a skydive.
(2) Understand how safety is engineered in real-life scenarios.

How to use the simulation:
Choose a height of the skydive and change the parachute diameter to understand when to open a parachute for a safe landing.

Qualitative understanding:
Why do you think jumping from 10,000 ft (3,300 m) is safer than jumping from 1,800 ft (600 m)?
At a safe velocity of 3.7 m/s, the minimum required parachute radius comes out to be approximately 4 m. Any radius smaller than this results in a parachute that is too small, regardless of the skydiver’s height. Why do you think that is the case?
In the output window, the terminal velocity noted is sometimes lower than the safe velocity. Still, the final velocity is not the terminal velocity—why do you think that is the case?

Quantitative understanding:
Choose a height of 3,300 m.
(i) Calculate the velocity of an object if it reaches the ground without any deceleration.
(ii) Calculate the terminal velocity of a 71 kg skydiver without a parachute assuming that the drag coefficient for a human is 1 and the skydiving arch position area is 0.6 m2. If the safe landing speed is 3.7 m/s, how much does this number come out to be? The density of air is 1 kg/m3.
(iii) Calculate the terminal velocity of a skydiver with a parachute assuming that the drag coefficient for the parachute is 1.75 and the parachute has a radius of 4 m. To simplify your calculations for this part, you can ignore the drag contribution of the human.
