Directions

This simulation shows how the partial pressures of the two components (A-blue, B-green) of a binary mixture change with mole fraction of component B. An ideal solution obeys Raoult's law; the partial pressure of each component is proportional to its mole fraction: PA = xA PAsat and PB = xB PBsat, where PsatA and PsatB are the saturation pressures of A and B at a given temperature. For an ideal solution, the total pressure P = xAPAsat + xBPBsat is shown by the black curve.

Most real solutions exhibit deviations from Raoult's law. For a positive deviation, PA > xAPAsat and PB > xBPBsat; this indicates that attractive interactions between like molecules is greater than between A and B molecules. For a negative deviation, PA < xAPAsat and PB < xBPBsat; this indicates that attractive interactions between like molecules is less than between A and B molecules.

When the slider for deviation from ideality is at a value other than zero, the non‑ideal behavior is shown by the solid lines and the dashed lines indicate ideal behavior. Even for non‑ideal solutions, Raoult's law is asymptotically approached for A as xA approaches 1 and for B as xB approaches 0.

Dilute solutions, on the other hand, are approximated by Henry's law: PA = xAKA for xA close to zero and PB = xBKB for xB close to zero. Check “Show Henry's law” to display this behavior represented by dashed purple and orange lines. Zoom with the scroll wheel and drag with the mouse. Move the cursor across the plot to read the values on each curve.
